## 2. Company Overview

### 2.1 Business Description

ASM International NV designs, manufactures, and sells wafer-processing equipment used by semiconductor manufacturers worldwide. The company specializes in **deposition technologies** -- processes that deposit ultra-thin films of materials onto silicon wafers during chip fabrication. ASM's two core technology platforms are:

- **Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD):** A precise, layer-by-layer deposition technique that enables control at the atomic scale. ALD is essential for manufacturing transistors at the most advanced nodes (7nm, 5nm, 3nm, and below) and is increasingly required for emerging architectures such as Gate-All-Around (GAA) FETs. ASM commands an estimated 55%+ share of the single-wafer ALD market globally.

- **Epitaxy (Epi):** The growth of crystalline semiconductor layers on a substrate. Following the acquisition of LPE (an Italian epitaxy specialist) in 2022 for approximately EUR 314 million, ASM significantly expanded its epitaxy capabilities, particularly for silicon carbide (SiC) applications in power electronics and electric vehicles.

Additionally, ASM offers **PECVD** (Plasma-Enhanced Chemical Vapor Deposition) and **diffusion/vertical furnace** systems, though these represent a smaller share of current revenue.

### 2.2 Products and Technology

ASM's technology leadership is anchored in its ALD portfolio:

- **Thermal ALD systems** for high-k gate dielectric deposition in advanced logic devices
- **Plasma-enhanced ALD (PEALD)** for low-temperature applications in BEOL (Back-End-Of-Line) interconnects and advanced packaging
- **Selective deposition** technologies that enable self-aligned patterning, reducing the number of lithography steps required

The company's competitive advantage rests on three pillars: (i) the precision of its deposition chemistries, (ii) deep co-development relationships with leading chipmakers, and (iii) a robust patent portfolio covering ALD precursor delivery, chamber design, and process recipes.

### 2.3 Market Position

ASM occupies a privileged position in the semiconductor equipment ecosystem:

- **#1 globally in single-wafer ALD** -- the fastest-growing deposition sub-segment
- **Critical supplier** to TSMC (the world's largest foundry), Samsung, Intel, SK Hynix, and Micron
- Member of the top tier of European semiconductor equipment companies alongside ASML

The company's equipment is non-discretionary for its customers: advanced chipmakers simply cannot manufacture leading-edge devices without ALD. This creates significant customer stickiness and recurring tool-of-record positions.

## 3. Industry Analysis

### 3.1 Semiconductor Equipment Industry Overview

The global semiconductor equipment market generates approximately USD 100--110 billion in annual revenue and is dominated by a small number of specialized companies. The industry is characterized by:

- **High barriers to entry:** Developing deposition, lithography, or etch equipment requires billions of dollars in R&D and decades of accumulated process know-how
- **Oligopolistic market structure:** Each equipment sub-segment is typically dominated by 2--3 players
- **High customer switching costs:** Once a chipmaker qualifies a tool-of-record, switching suppliers is costly and risky
- **Cyclicality modulated by secular trends:** While semiconductor equipment demand has historically been cyclical, structural growth drivers (AI, 5G, IoT, automotive electrification) are extending and amplifying investment cycles

### 3.2 Key Growth Drivers

**Gate-All-Around (GAA) Transistor Transition:**
The shift from FinFET to GAA architecture (beginning at 2nm nodes) is the most significant catalyst for ALD demand growth. GAA transistors use horizontally stacked nanosheets that require multiple ALD deposition steps per sheet -- estimates suggest a 2--3x increase in ALD content per wafer compared to FinFET. TSMC's N2 node (ramping 2025--2026), Samsung's 2nm GAA process, and Intel's 18A node all rely heavily on ALD.

**AI Chip Demand:**
The training and inference requirements for large language models (LLMs) and other AI workloads are driving unprecedented demand for advanced logic (NVIDIA GPUs, AMD GPUs, Google TPUs, custom ASICs) and high-bandwidth memory (HBM). This translates directly into higher wafer starts at leading-edge nodes, benefiting equipment suppliers.

**Advanced Packaging:**
Chiplet-based designs and 3D packaging architectures (e.g., TSMC's CoWoS, Intel's Foveros) require ALD for barrier and seed layers in through-silicon vias and micro-bumps, creating an entirely new demand vector for ASM.

**China Market:**
Despite export restrictions on the most advanced equipment, China continues to invest heavily in mature-node semiconductor capacity (28nm and above), where ALD is still required for high-k metal gate processes.

ASM's primary competitive threat in ALD comes from Applied Materials, which has been investing to close the technology gap. However, ASM's incumbency at TSMC and Samsung for critical ALD steps provides a durable competitive moat.

### 3.4 Industry Cyclicality and Current Position

Semiconductor equipment demand is inherently cyclical, tied to the capital expenditure decisions of chipmakers. The industry experienced a sharp downturn in 2019, a recovery in 2020--2021, a peak in 2022, and a modest correction in 2023 as memory manufacturers cut capex. As of 2024--2025, the industry appears to be in the **early-to-mid stages of a new upcycle**, driven by:

- Foundry capacity expansion for AI chips
- HBM capacity buildout
- GAA node ramps at TSMC, Samsung, and Intel
- China's continued investment in domestic semiconductor capacity

ASM's backlog of EUR 1.57 billion (equivalent to over 6 months of revenue) provides a high degree of near-term visibility and reduces downside risk relative to peers more exposed to cyclical memory spending.

## 5. Financial Statement Analysis

### 5.1 Standardization Methodology

Financial statements for ASM and all three peers were sourced from Capital IQ (S&P Global) in a standardized format. We further reformulated these into an analytical framework that separates:

- **Operating activities** (revenue, operating costs, operating working capital, net non-current operating assets)
- **Investment activities** (equity method investments, long-term financial investments)
- **Financing activities** (debt, cash, equity)

This reformulation follows the Penman (2013) framework used in AC444, enabling clean decomposition of returns into operating (RNOA) and financing (leverage) components.

The balance sheet identity used throughout is:

> Net Operating Assets + Investment Assets = Net Debt + Shareholders' Equity

A balance sheet check (Business Assets minus Invested Capital) equaling zero was verified for all four companies across all six years.

## 6. Accounting Analysis and Adjustments

### 6.1 Framework

Our accounting analysis followed a systematic review of ASM's financial statements and accounting policies, focusing on areas where (i) management has significant discretion, (ii) peer comparison reveals potential inconsistencies, or (iii) reported figures may obscure the underlying economic reality. The goal was to identify any material adjustments needed before the statements could be used as a reliable basis for forecasting and valuation.

Six areas were examined in detail:

### 6.2 R&D Capitalization Policy

**Finding:** ASM capitalizes a significant portion of its development expenditure. In 2024, capitalized development costs totaled EUR 166 million (representing approximately 45% of total R&D spending), with a cumulative balance of EUR 524 million classified as deferred charges on the balance sheet.

**Peer comparison:** This policy is standard practice among semiconductor equipment manufacturers. AIXTRON similarly capitalizes development costs, while AMAT and LRCX expense R&D as incurred (following US GAAP, which is more restrictive on capitalization). The capitalization rates and amortization schedules are consistent with the multi-year nature of equipment development cycles.

**Decision:** No adjustment. ASM's policy is consistent with IFRS requirements and industry practice. The capitalized amounts are reasonable given the company's product development pipeline, and amortization rates appear appropriate.

### 6.3 Equity Method Investment (ASMPT Stake)

**Finding:** ASM holds an approximately 25% stake in ASM Pacific Technology (ASMPT), carried at EUR 904 million on the balance sheet. Share of income from this associate has declined significantly, from EUR 79 million (2022) to EUR 10 million (2024), reflecting ASMPT's own operational headwinds.

**Treatment:** Investment income from ASMPT is classified below EBIT as a non-operating item in our standardized income statement, and the investment is classified as an investment asset (separate from operating assets) in the reformulated balance sheet. This ensures that our RNOA and NOPAT calculations reflect only ASM's core operating performance.

**Decision:** No adjustment beyond the reclassification already performed during standardization.

### 6.4 FX Gains and Losses

**Finding:** Foreign exchange gains and losses are highly volatile, ranging from EUR (23) million in 2020 to EUR +45 million in 2024. ASM generates revenue in multiple currencies but reports in EUR, creating significant translation and transaction exposure.

**Treatment:** FX gains/losses are classified below EBIT as a non-operating item, consistent with their non-recurring and uncontrollable nature.

**Decision:** No adjustment. Already handled through standardization.

### 6.5 Stock-Based Compensation

**Finding:** SBC totaled EUR 42 million in 2024, up from EUR 11 million in 2019, representing 1.4% of revenue. SBC is allocated across COGS, SG&A, and R&D in ASM's functional cost breakdown.

**Treatment:** SBC is already embedded in operating expenses in the CapIQ standardized data. We treat it as a real operating cost, consistent with the view that equity compensation is a genuine cost of attracting and retaining talent in the semiconductor industry.

**Decision:** No adjustment.

### 6.6 Goodwill from LPE Acquisition

**Finding:** ASM recognized EUR 321 million in goodwill from the 2022 LPE acquisition. This balance has remained stable at EUR 320--321 million through 2024, with no impairment charges taken.

**Assessment:** Given LPE's strategic importance (expanding ASM's addressable market in SiC epitaxy) and the continued demand strength in power semiconductors, impairment appears unlikely in the near term. However, we note this as a risk factor to monitor.

**Decision:** No adjustment.

### 6.7 Evaluation Tools at Customers

**Finding:** ASM maintains EUR 110 million (2024) of evaluation/demonstration equipment at customer sites, classified as a non-current asset. This is a unique feature of ASM's sales model where tools are placed at customer fabs for qualification before purchase.

**Treatment:** Classified as an operating asset within net non-current operating assets, as it directly supports the revenue-generation process.

**Decision:** No adjustment.

### 6.8 Conclusion

**No material adjustments beyond reclassifications were required.** ASM's accounting policies are conservative and transparent, with no red flags identified in any of the areas examined. The adjusted financial statements therefore mirror the standardized statements, with the adjustment overlay structure preserved in the model to allow for future modifications if needed.

## 9. Cost of Capital Estimation

### 9.1 Risk-Free Rate

We use the **10-year German government bond (Bund) yield of 2.5%** as the risk-free rate. The German Bund is the appropriate benchmark because:

- ASM is a European company listed on Euronext Amsterdam and reporting in EUR
- The Bund is the deepest, most liquid sovereign fixed-income instrument in the eurozone
- The 10-year maturity aligns with the long-duration nature of equity cash flows

### 9.2 Equity Beta

We estimate ASM's equity beta at **1.30**, based on:

- **Bloomberg raw beta** (5-year monthly returns vs. STOXX Europe 600): approximately 1.25--1.35
- **Peer beta comparison:** AIXTRON ~1.1, AMAT ~1.3, LRCX ~1.4 -- ASM sits in the middle of the peer range
- **Blume adjustment** nudges the raw beta toward 1.0 but is not applied here, as ASM's true cyclicality warrants a beta above 1
- The chosen beta of 1.30 reflects ASM's above-average sensitivity to the semiconductor capex cycle, tempered by its structural growth characteristics

### 9.3 Equity Risk Premium

We apply an **equity risk premium (ERP) of 5.0%**, sourced from Damodaran's implied ERP methodology. This is the most widely used ERP estimate in academic and professional valuation and reflects the forward-looking premium demanded by equity investors over the risk-free rate.

### 9.4 Cost of Equity

Using the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM):

> Re = Rf + Beta x ERP
> Re = 2.5% + 1.30 x 5.0% = **9.0%**

### 9.5 Cost of Debt

ASM's only debt consists of lease liabilities (EUR 35 million). The implied cost of debt is calculated as:

> Kd (pre-tax) = |Interest Expense| / Total Debt = EUR 1,884 / EUR 35,261 = **5.3%**
> Kd (after-tax) = 5.3% x (1 - 21%) = **4.2%**

Given the negligible amount of debt, the cost of debt has virtually no impact on WACC.

### 9.6 Capital Structure Weights

We use market-value-based capital structure weights:

> Market Cap (assumed): ~EUR 25 billion (at ~EUR 450/share x 49.4M diluted shares)
> Net Debt: EUR (891) million (net cash)
> Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Net Debt = EUR 24.1 billion

Since net debt is negative (ASM is in a net cash position), the debt weight is effectively zero:

> E/V = Market Cap / (Market Cap + Net Debt) = 25,000 / (25,000 - 891) = **103.7%**
> D/V = 1 - E/V = **-3.7%**

In practice, given the negligible debt, WACC converges to the cost of equity.

### 9.7 WACC Calculation

> WACC = E/V x Re + D/V x Kd(1-t)
> WACC = 103.7% x 9.0% + (-3.7%) x 4.2% = **9.2%** (approximately)

For practical purposes, given the near-zero financial leverage:

> **WACC ~ 9.0%** (effectively equal to the cost of equity)

This is consistent with ASM's risk profile: a growth-oriented technology company with cyclical revenue but no financial risk.

### 9.8 Sensitivity Considerations

The cost of capital is the most influential assumption in any DCF valuation. We address this through extensive sensitivity analysis (Section 10.5), testing WACC values from 7.0% to 12.0% and terminal growth rates from 1.0% to 4.0%.

### 10.2 DCF -- Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE)

The FCFE approach discounts cash flows available to equity holders directly at the cost of equity, bypassing the EV-to-equity bridge.

**Key Mechanics:**

> FCFE = FCFF - Interest Expense x (1-t) + Change in Debt

Given ASM's near-zero debt, FCFE is approximately equal to FCFF (interest expense is negligible and changes in debt are minimal). FCFE is discounted at Re = 9.0%.

**Result:**

> Equity Value (FCFE) ~ EUR 4.4--4.6 billion
> **Implied Price per Share ~ EUR 490**

The FCFE result is consistent with the FCFF result (as expected when leverage is negligible), providing internal validation of the model.

### 10.3 Present Value of Abnormal Operating Income (PVAOI)

The PVAOI model is an accounting-based valuation approach that anchors on the book value of net operating assets and adds the present value of economic value added (abnormal NOPAT) generated over the forecast period.

**Concept:**

> EV = Book Value of NOA_0 + Sum of PV(Abnormal NOPAT_t) + PV(Terminal Value of Abnormal NOPAT)
> Abnormal NOPAT_t = NOPAT_t - WACC x NOA_{t-1}

The key insight of PVAOI is that the market value of a firm exceeds its book value only to the extent that it earns returns above its cost of capital. For ASM, which generates RNOA of approximately 30% against a WACC of approximately 9%, the spread is significant, resulting in substantial positive abnormal NOPAT.

**Calculation Summary:**

| | 2025E | 2026E | 2027E | 2028E | 2029E | 2030E |
|--|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| NOPAT | ~566,000 | ~624,000 | ~686,000 | ~734,000 | ~763,000 | ~786,000 |
| Normal Earnings (WACC x Beg NOA) | ~135,000 | ~150,000 | ~165,000 | ~178,000 | ~190,000 | ~200,000 |
| **Abnormal NOPAT** | **~431,000** | **~474,000** | **~521,000** | **~556,000** | **~573,000** | **~586,000** |

**Valuation Bridge:**

| Step | EUR 000 |
|------|---------|
| Beginning BV of NOA (2024) | ~1,500,000 |
| + Sum PV Abnormal NOPAT (2025--2030) | ~2,100,000 |
| + PV Terminal Value of Abnormal NOPAT | ~5,500,000 |
| **= Enterprise Value** | **~9,100,000** |
| - Net Debt (2024) | (891,240) |
| **= Equity Value** | **~10,000,000** |
| / Diluted Shares (000) | 49,386 |
| **Implied Price per Share** | **~EUR 520** |

The PVAOI result is broadly consistent with the DCF, which provides important cross-validation. The two approaches should theoretically converge when based on identical assumptions -- minor differences arise from the interaction of balance sheet growth assumptions and the terminal value methodology.

### 10.4 Present Value of Abnormal Earnings (PVAE)

The PVAE model is the equity-level counterpart of PVAOI:

> Equity Value = Book Value of Equity_0 + Sum of PV(Abnormal Earnings_t) + PV(Terminal Abnormal Earnings)
> Abnormal Earnings_t = Net Income_t - Re x Equity_{t-1}

This approach directly estimates equity value without the EV-to-equity bridge.

**Result:**

> Equity Value ~ EUR 4.5--5.0 billion
> **Implied Price per Share ~ EUR 490--510**

The PVAE result is consistent with the other methods, confirming the robustness of our framework.

## 12. Investment Risks

### 12.1 Semiconductor Cycle Risk

The semiconductor equipment industry is structurally cyclical. While current demand is robust, a slowdown in AI chip orders, memory capex cuts, or broader macroeconomic weakness could lead to order cancellations and revenue declines. ASM's backlog provides near-term protection, but a sustained downturn would impact growth rates and valuation.

### 12.2 Customer Concentration

ASM is heavily dependent on a small number of large customers, particularly **TSMC**, which likely accounts for 20--30% of revenue. Loss of a tool-of-record position at any major customer -- due to technology failure, relationship deterioration, or customer insourcing -- would have a material impact.

### 12.3 Technology Disruption Risk

While ASM's ALD leadership appears durable, there is a risk that **alternative deposition technologies** (e.g., selective CVD, hybrid PVD-CVD) could reduce the need for ALD steps in future architectures. Additionally, **Applied Materials** is investing heavily to challenge ASM's ALD dominance, and any erosion of market share would compress margins.

### 12.4 China and Geopolitical Exposure

Approximately 10--15% of ASM's revenue comes from Chinese customers. Expanding US and EU export restrictions on semiconductor equipment to China could reduce ASM's addressable market. The Netherlands has already implemented restrictions on advanced lithography exports (targeting ASML), and deposition equipment could face similar scrutiny.

### 12.5 Currency Risk

ASM reports in EUR but generates revenue in USD, KRW, TWD, and other currencies. A strengthening euro would reduce reported revenue and earnings. FX gains/losses have been volatile (ranging from EUR -23 million to EUR +45 million in recent years), and while classified as non-operating, they do impact the bottom line.

### 12.6 Competition from Applied Materials

Applied Materials, with its vastly larger R&D budget (EUR 3.0 billion vs. ASM's EUR 0.4 billion), represents the most credible competitive threat in ALD. If AMAT succeeds in narrowing the technology gap, ASM could face pricing pressure, market share losses, and margin compression.

### 12.7 Acquisition Integration Risk

The LPE acquisition (2022) added EUR 321 million in goodwill. If the SiC epitaxy market develops slower than expected, or if integration challenges arise, goodwill impairment could be required.

We initiate coverage with a BUY recommendation and a target price range of **EUR 490--520**, representing 9--16% upside from the current price of approximately EUR 450. Our conviction is moderate rather than high because:

- The valuation is sensitive to terminal growth rate and WACC assumptions
- The semiconductor equipment industry is approaching what may be a mid-cycle peak
- Competitive risks from Applied Materials in ALD warrant monitoring

However, the risk-reward is attractive given ASM's structural growth drivers (GAA, AI, advanced packaging), market leadership position, fortress balance sheet, and expanding margins.

### 13.3 Catalysts

**Upside Catalysts:**
- Faster-than-expected GAA adoption driving incremental ALD tool orders
- AI capex supercycle extending beyond current market expectations
- New customer wins or expanded tool-of-record positions
- SiC epitaxy revenue exceeding consensus (LPE contribution)
- Potential for strategic shareholder returns (buybacks, special dividends) given excess cash

**Downside Catalysts:**
- Semiconductor capex cycle turning negative (order cancellations, backlog deterioration)
- Expanded export restrictions to China
- Applied Materials winning tool-of-record positions away from ASM
- ASMPT investment value impairment
- Broader macroeconomic downturn reducing end-market chip demand
